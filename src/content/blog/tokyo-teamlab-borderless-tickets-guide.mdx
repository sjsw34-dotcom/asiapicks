## What Is teamLab Borderless?

**teamLab** is a Tokyo-based interdisciplinary art collective that has been redefining what immersive digital art looks like since 2001. Their *Borderless* concept is the flagship experience: a massive, labyrinthine space where dozens of artworks have no fixed location and constantly move and interact with one another — and with you.

The original teamLab Borderless opened in Odaiba in 2018 and attracted over 2.3 million visitors in its first year, making it one of the most visited single-artist museum exhibitions in the world. After a temporary closure and relocation, the **new teamLab Borderless** reopened in **Azabudai Hills** in early 2024, bringing a completely redesigned experience across a sprawling underground venue in one of Tokyo's most exciting new districts.

This isn't teamLab Planets (that's a different, also incredible experience in Toyosu). Borderless is larger, more complex, and more disorienting — in the best possible way.

## Location, Getting There & Opening Hours

### Address & Location

The new teamLab Borderless is located at:

**teamLab Borderless, Azabudai Hills**
1-2-4 Azabudai, Minato-ku, Tokyo 106-0041

Azabudai Hills is a brand-new mixed-use development that opened in late 2023 and has quickly become one of Tokyo's most talked-about destinations. The teamLab venue sits in the basement of the complex.

### Getting There

Getting to Azabudai Hills is straightforward from most central Tokyo locations:

- **Tokyo Metro Hibiya Line** → Kamiyacho Station (Exit 3) — approximately 5-minute walk
- **Tokyo Metro Namboku Line / Toei Oedo Line** → Roppongi-itchome Station — approximately 8-minute walk
- **Toei Asakusa Line / Oedo Line** → Daimon Station with a short taxi or bus connection

If you're staying in popular tourist neighborhoods like Shinjuku, Shibuya, or Ginza, you're looking at roughly **20–30 minutes by train**. Booking a hotel in the Roppongi or Toranomon area on **Agoda** puts you within easy walking distance and makes evening visits a breeze.

### Where to Buy Tickets

**Book in advance — this cannot be overstated.** teamLab Borderless regularly sells out, especially on weekends, public holidays, and during Japan's peak travel seasons (cherry blossom in late March/April and autumn foliage in November).

Your best options:

1. **Official teamLab website** (borderless.teamlab.art) — Buy directly with a specific entry time slot
2. **Klook** — Often has bundled deals and English-language support, making it ideal for international travelers
3. **On-site** — Available only if slots haven't sold out, which is a risk not worth taking

Booking through **Klook** is particularly convenient if you're already using the platform to organize other Tokyo activities. You can manage everything in one place and often find combo deals with other digital art experiences or city tours.

## What to Expect Inside

### The Art Worlds

teamLab Borderless is divided into several interconnected "worlds," though the layout is intentionally maze-like and changes constantly. Some of the standout experiences include:

- **Forest of Resonating Lamps** — Thousands of glass spheres light up in response to your presence, creating waves of color that ripple outward
- **Borderless World** — The main sprawling space where artworks flow between rooms and interact with each other in real time
- **Athletics Forest** — A physically immersive space designed for children and adults alike, with climbing structures and reactive environments
- **EN TEA HOUSE: Camellias** — A tea ceremony experience where flowers bloom in your cup; yes, it's as magical as it sounds
- **Sketch Aquarium** — Color in a sea creature on paper, and watch it come to life swimming in a digital ocean

### What Borderless Actually Means

The defining feature of teamLab Borderless is that *nothing is fixed*. Artworks aren't contained in individual rooms — they spill across walls, floors, ceilings, and into other artworks. The digital koi you see in one space might swim through a wall into the next room. It creates a sense of genuine wonder that's hard to replicate and impossible to fully capture on a phone screen (though you'll absolutely try).

## teamLab Borderless vs. teamLab Planets

A common question from first-time visitors: *should I do Borderless or Planets?* Here's a quick breakdown:

| | teamLab Borderless | teamLab Planets |
|--|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Location** | Azabudai Hills, Minato | Toyosu, Koto |
| **Size** | Very large, labyrinthine | Smaller, more focused |
| **Experience** | Exploratory, no set path | Linear, guided flow |
| **Water element** | No | Yes (wade through water) |
| **Time needed** | 2–3 hours | 1–1.5 hours |
| **Best for** | Art lovers, explorers | All ages, quick visit |

Honestly? If you have the time and budget, do both. They're genuinely different experiences and each is worth the ticket price on its own.

## Practical Tips for Your Visit

These are the things you'll wish someone had told you before you arrived:

- **Go on a weekday morning** — The space is significantly less crowded, and you'll actually be able to sit and absorb the art rather than shuffle through with a crowd
- **Wear comfortable, non-slip socks** — You'll be asked to remove your shoes in some areas. Socks with grip are ideal; bare feet are fine but socks keep things hygienic
- **Avoid white or light-colored clothing** — The projections look stunning, but the UV light in some rooms will make white clothes look blinding and can wash out your photos
- **Charge your phone beforehand** — You will take more photos and videos than you've ever taken in a single day
- **Allow 2–3 hours minimum** — Rushing through teamLab Borderless is genuinely wasteful. Give yourself time to get lost
- **Download the teamLab app** — It provides maps and additional interactive features that enhance certain installations
- **Check the weather** — The venue is indoors and climate controlled, so it's a perfect rainy day activity in Tokyo

## Getting the Most Out of Azabudai Hills

Since you're already in Azabudai Hills, it's worth spending time in the broader complex. The development includes high-end restaurants, boutique shops, and some genuinely beautiful public art spaces. It's a great area to grab dinner after your teamLab visit — the area has options ranging from Japanese ramen to international cuisine at various price points.

If you're planning a multi-day Tokyo itinerary that includes teamLab, Roppongi Hills (home of the Mori Art Museum) is a short walk away and makes for a natural pairing on an art-focused day in the city.
